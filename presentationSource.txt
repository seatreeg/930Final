\begin{frame}{Meet the Domain Experts}
Anna Campbell and Shayla Meyer are both dental students working on a research project as part of a requirement for the completion of their dental program.

The project includes data collection, a poster presentation, and a written report.

Campbell and Meyer had decided to base their project on another group's project last year, which had compared the mental health of 4 dental school grades using three mental health metrics.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Question of the Former Study}
In the 2023–2024 academic year, do dental school grades show significant differences from each other in the three mental health measures?

\begin{columns}
    \column{0.75\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/oldStudy.png}
\end{columns}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Survey Setup}
Several QR code posters were placed around the UNMC Dental College.

Students responding to the survey would answer a set of mental health questions from three questionnaires.

The project used data from last year and this year. Identifiable information was not stored, which means that if a student had taken the survey this year and last year, there is no way to tell.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Metrics}

\begin{columns}
    \column{0.75\textwidth}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Patient Health Questionnaire 9 (PHQ-9): 9-question survey designed to quickly assess a respondent's risk of depression. 0 to 3 integer scale for each question.
        \item Burnout Mini-Z 1.0 Q3: Single question asking survey respondents to report how burned out they feel. 0 to 4 integer scale.
        \item Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7 (GAD-7): 7-question survey designed to quickly assess a respondent's risk of generalized anxiety disorder. 0 to 3 integer scale for each question.
    \end{itemize}

    \column{0.25\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/phqTest.PNG}
\end{columns}

\vspace{0.25cm}

All of these scores are designed to be easily graded using a point system, where the points from each question are added together to get a total value.

\end{frame}



\begin{frame}{Terms}
\textbf{D1, D2, D3, D4}: Codes indicating what year in dental school a student is, similar to words like “Freshman” and “Senior”.

\textbf{(2023–2024) / (2024–2025)}: Which academic year the survey was taken.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Responding to Client Goals}
Client Goals:
\begin{itemize}
\item Analysis should be valid and reasonable (as always).
\item No expectation of any specific result; client just needed to perform a research project.
\item No issue with complex methods, but would like to take time to understand the methods used.
\item The results should be prepared for a presentation.
\end{itemize}

Actions:
\begin{itemize}
\item Be careful and transparent about validity conditions and choices.
\item Prefer robust methods when possible.
\item If equally valid, prefer the less complex method.
\item Provide mini-lessons to explain methods.
\item Summarize choices in short sentences for presentation.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Questions of Interest}
\begin{itemize}
\item In the 2024–2025 academic year, do any dental school grades show significant differences from each other across the three mental health measures? For example, is the average senior different from the average freshman?
\item In terms of the three mental health metrics, is the average dental school student of a given grade different this year from last year? Are freshman this year less or more anxious on average?
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Subjective Judgment Call}
%Diagnostic plots have a subjective component and there is no definitive result from these plots. Even with testing that provides a numeric result, it's subjective whether or not the results of tests, such as Levene's test for homogeneity of variance or the Shapiro-Wilk test for normality, are overly conservative.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Diagnostic plots are somewhat subjective.
        \item Tests that produce a numeric result are still somewhat subjective, as the tests of normality (Shapiro-Wilk) and unequal variances (Levene's test) are sometimes considered overly-conservative depending on the sample size.
    \end{itemize}

\textbf{Ultimately, I made the decision to NOT assume normality or equality of variance within groups.}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Non-Parametric Issue}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Originally, Kruskal Wallis and Dunns test were considered.
        \item This would substantially change the hypothesis we are interested in.
        \item Many non-parametric tests like KW are sensitive to distributional differences that are not the location, which causes further issues for the hypothesis.
        \item To stay consistent with the client’s hypothesis, a different method would be required.
    \end{itemize}
%At first, the plan was to use the Kruskal Wallis (KW) and Dunns test as a non-parametric alternatives to F and t testing.
%These tests are more so related to the medians as a measurement of location instead of the mean and are also sensitive to other, non-location distributional differences that the client is not interested.
%Ultimately, this would require them to reframe the hypothesis as one of multiple distribution differences, and not just a difference in means
%To stay consistent with the client’s hypothesis, a different method would be required.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Bootstrapping and Adjustment Meeting}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Bootstrapping was chosen as a non-parametric alternative that preserves the main idea the client is interested in testing.
        \item To reduce the family-wise error rate, a Bonferroni adjustment was used.
        \item The choice of bootstrap and the importance of adjusting the p-values was communicated to the client.
    \end{itemize}
%Using a bootstrapping method, we can perform a test that does not assume normality and is testing for our parameter of interest. 
%We also are performing multiple tests for the same family of questions, so we need to make an adjustment to our p-values.
\end{frame}
